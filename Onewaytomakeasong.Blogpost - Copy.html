One way to make a good song

Start with some solid poetry

I like it when the chorus of a song is a little bit poetic and has some meaning

It's a good idea to not make it too specific. It leaves room for interpretation

This is a good time to come up with a lyrical theme or idea for the song.

Try to sing a melody with this poetry and try to make it a little catchy

You might have to edit the syllables or rhyme pattern to make it more catchy and flow

Now focus on adding a bass line that fits the vibe of the lyrics and melody

If you want to add variation to the arrangement now is a good time to have a secondary and tertiary bass lines for verse and bridge etc

Now flush out those lyrical ideas with the respective bass lines.

Reference the song writing cheat sheet to help write lyrics.

Think about variation with the rhythm of the bass, or you could worry about that later.

NOTE: Don't underestimate the importance of sound selection.

The instruments you choose early on dictate the direction of the sonic vibes.

NOTE: If you're really lacking in lyrics, consider having a post-chorus drop to fill out that time (which you can build in later)

For now you can focus on what chords you'll have. It should be a lot easier now that you have melodic content from the vocals and a bass line.

You just have to fill the space in between.

Chords are pretty simple when you break it down.

Consider inversions, 7ths and 9ths, chromatic walk down/up chords, tension

Rhythmic anticipation, rhythmic delay

Arpeggiation

Depending on how complex your chords are, you might want to add other harmonic and/or melodic layers using other instruments.

A few ideas for some layers: Counter melody, Pad, Arpeggio, Plucks!

The key idea is to fill out the sonic spectrum, different pitches, rhythms, and timbres.

For example: if you have a short plucky sound that has a quick rhythm, try adding a long pad that plays a slower rhythm.

Also consider how you might want to add variation by having different instruments for different sections

(this is a good/easy way to add variation especially if you only have one chord progression for the entire song)

Extra melodic/harmonic tips:

Panning!

Consider some fx, Reverse, Reverse pitch delay, etc

Granular synthesis >.>

Other resampling magic? idk

cheap trick!: switch to a higher octave for the chorus to add that energy!

(warning, this may create a frequency void that needs to be filled)

At this point you should have a really strong harmonic/melodic foundation. Now it's time to focus on the percussion aspect.

Not every song has drums. Consider the album blonde by Frank Ocean; many of the songs don't have drums.

Not having drums emphasizes the dreamy vibes and allows u to focus more on the atmosphere and ambient textures.

But if you really want a hit song, drums are key.

Drums can help guide the rhythm of the bass line, and vice versa.

Theres a few places to start with drums. First you could try to experiment with a few drum loops to see what vibes work

If you're lucky you can find a loop you really like and chop it up to add a little variation,

and layer it with some one shots and add effects to spice it up

Another technique is starting with one shots

You could try adding a perc loop or create your own perc loop using a glitchscape

Extra perc tips:

Panning! :)

Velocity/Volume is really important

Adding swing is a good way to get a funky bounce

Shaperbox is really good for adding movement, fx, or a little noise.

Reversed perc sounds can really add some impact

Experiment with some fx like delay, convolution, stretching, bitcrush, etc.

Pitch shifting drums sounds can keep it interesting

Silence is a great way to add rhythm and break up the melody

Try changing the tempo all together

You could also get a little experimental with polyrhythmic patterns

Consider side-chaining the kick ;)

Try messing with transients and decay time.

Above all, don't be afraid to keep it simple, the key is to have a good sound selection.

NOTE: if you're lacking inspiration, try listening to some good music :)

At this point, you're really deep in. Take a break. Step back and consider some key ideas:

Mixing, Texture, Arrangement, Melody, Rhythm, Purpose, Story, Transitions, Contrast, Cohesion.

If you haven't already, now might be a good time to make sure you have enough vocal layers/harmonies, before things get to crazy.

Potential layers: main center vocal, L/R vocal doubles, background vocal with reverb,

multiple harmonies, ad-libs or secondary lyrics, different voices(whispering - screaming), vocoding!

Make sure you adjust panning, and add different fx / eq to get the layers to sit in the mix and add some style.

It's important to treat your voice like an instrument. Know it's limitations.

Remember, keep it simple. Once you record your vocal layers consider taking some of it out :)

Another thing to consider is side-chaining the fx, especially for the main vocal. It helps the vocal to stand out.

Before you add too much texture, it's a good time to consider resampling what you got to create some variation with low effort.

Resampling is also a great way to create transitions (using reverse echos)

Other resampling ideas: Blurring, Chopping, Time stretching, Pitch shifting, Reversing.

Finish the arrangement: At this point, you should have a strong foundation for your song.

Try adding any additional layers or effects that you think will enhance the overall sound.

Noise:

Noise can be very thematic and add movie vibes or give character to the music

Or it can be much more subtle to add a little grit or vintagey warmth

SFX:

textural sfx can be similar to noise but more sparse and just add to the atmosphere

alternatively you can add more umph to your percussion. Something you cant quite do with effects or perc samples

I differentiate percs and SFX. The key difference in my opinion is that SFX can act like a perc but they add more character

and a little bit more texture. In a way it can really emphasize a certain emotion. Percs often have less tonality to them compared to SFX.

For example, silly cartoon sfx can make things more funny.

While percs are more foundational to the drums as a whole.

SFX allows for a connection between thematic/emotions to the percussion aspect of the song

This is similar to the concept of how the bass line can really connect the melodic/harmonic ideas to the rhythmic ideas.

Producer tags/ intro voice lines:

These can make your intros really iconic if thats the vibe you're going for.

It's a great opportunity to add your own unique stamp that can get u recognized.

It also kinda acts as a cool transition sound and give off DJ set vibes, a great way to break up an album.

Ask yourself how are different elements connecting to one another?

Perhaps through another element, or through harmonic or rhythmic contrast, through key, through textural theme or timbre, perhaps Psychoacoustically.

MIX/MASTER!:

Solve problems you find, don't go looking for problems. There is a difference.

Gain Stage!

Mix in mono

Mix at low volume

Analyze frequencies

Analyze stereo field

Dynamic Eq by side-chaining freq bands

Cut lows and highs

Use Maximus to widen mids and narrow lows

Separate channel for fx on vocals to sidechain to main vocal

Compare to reference tracks

Parallel Processing(distortion,compression,etc.)

Multiband Compression

Multiband Saturation

Panning

Use Patcher to mid-side EQ - try boosting high freq on side eq and/or dipping boxy mids on mid

some amateur mistakes:

Phase canceling your bass with other instruments like guitar. Side chain that baby so the guitar can have bass when there is no bass

Putting too much reverb on drums - especially snares >.>

Mixing is an essential part of the music production process. It allows you to shape and sculpt the individual tracks in your project into a cohesive and polished final product. There are a few key concepts to keep in mind when mixing to help you achieve the best possible results.

First and foremost, it's important to focus on solving problems rather than looking for them. In other words, don't try to fix things that aren't broken. Instead, identify any issues with your mix and take steps to address them. This could include cutting excess low or high frequencies, using dynamic EQ to sidechain certain frequency bands, or using parallel processing to add saturation or distortion to specific tracks.

Another important concept to consider when mixing is gain staging. This refers to the process of setting the level of each track in your mix so that it's neither too loud nor too quiet. Proper gain staging ensures that you have enough headroom to work with and can help prevent distortion and other issues.

Mixing in mono can also be a helpful technique, as it allows you to focus on the overall balance and clarity of your mix rather than getting caught up in stereo effects. Similarly, mixing at a low volume can help you avoid fatigue and make better mixing decisions.

Analyzing frequencies and the stereo field can also be useful when mixing. This can help you identify imbalances in your mix and take steps to correct them. For example, you might use a multiband compressor to even out the levels of different frequency ranges or use a mid-side EQ to boost the high frequencies on the sides of your mix.[a]

Using reference tracks is another useful technique when mixing. By comparing your mix to tracks that you admire, you can get a better sense of how your mix stacks up and identify areas where you might need to make adjustments.

Finally, don't forget about panning, which allows you to position different elements of your mix in the stereo field. This can help create a sense of depth and width in your mix and can be especially effective when used in combination with other mixing techniques such as multiband compression and saturation.

Some common mistakes that amateur mixers might make include phase canceling the bass with other instruments like guitar, putting too much reverb on drums, especially snares, and failing to use sidechaining to control the levels of different tracks. By avoiding these pitfalls and keeping these mixing concepts in mind, you'll be well on your way to creating professional-sounding mixes.
